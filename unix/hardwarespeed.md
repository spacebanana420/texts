# Your Hardware is Very fast

Computers are incredibly fast, but many people upgrade their PC hardware because it starts feeling slow over the years, but is it really slow? Absolutely not.
Our computers are overwhelmingly fast but their speed is hindered by slow, inefficient and bloated software.
There's a few ways in how this affects us and how we can fix it.


## Operating system bloat

Proprietary operating systems like Windows and MacOS have become significantly slower.
A modern CPU can feel sluggish on Windows 11, you can test this by lowering the clock speed.
As you lower the CPU clock speed, the Windows 10/11 desktop and some of its software (like the explorer) become much slower.

Open source operating systems and UNIX-like operating systems prioritise a different culture and mentality of software around freedom and efficiency.
Out of the box, they tend to be faster and use fewer resources, but if they didn't you could configure them to do so.

On Linux systems, whether I use Xfce, Openbox, Cinnamon, Hyprland or Wayfire, with or without animations these desktops are blazingly fast.
So fast that I can set my CPU clock speed to abysmally low levels like 400MHz and the desktops still respond to my inputs instantly and are very fast.
This also applies to the software you use here, such as file explorers and the likes.


## Software bloat

Software, both proprietary and open source also tends to be slower and less efficient.
It's all a matter of choice: Windows file explorer is slow, but other file managers like Dolphin and Nautilus are notably faster, and others like Thunar and PCmanFM are crazy fast.
Some are even faster if they are TUI like `lf` and `nnn`, and managing files from a shell is the fastest in terms of resource usage.

Sometimes a certain task is more efficient when done from the terminal, then you can also leverage its performance benefits, but there's GUI software that is lightweight too.


## Types of bloat

Your software can be unnecessarily inefficient and consume too many resources in various ways:
* Startup time is too slow
* Individual tasks inside the software can take too long for what they do
* Software feels slow and unresponsible in its interface or while using it
* Software is too large (disk usage) for what it does
* Software uses too much RAM for what it does
* Software uses too much CPU in the background
* Software does too much compared to what you actually want it to do


## Eco-friendly

Once you unlock your hardware's potential with a system and software that are lightweight, you lose most of your necessity for upgrading your PC.
You upgrade much less often, only when truly necessary, and you can get away with older or refurbished hardware.


## Conclusion

Our hardware is very fast, and our perception that it's slow comes from slow software.
Proprietary operating systems and many applications are slow, and many notably faster and lighter alternatives exist.
The moment you switch you realise how fast your hardware really is, then you will only ever need an upgrade for when you start playing heavier games or doing heavier tasks like media encoding and rendering.
