# From Broken to Tree

Plants and trees are very versatile and can adapt to many different circumstances. If you cut a plant or a tree, both separate parts can survive on their own. The log will grow new branches, and the broken branch will grow new roots.

I have a tree at home, approximatedly 1.5m tall, and one day one of my cats broke a branch. Instead of tossing away, I decided to re-plant it, but at first I used water. I had never taken care of plants with purely water and no dirt and wasn't very aware of the concept of hydroponics, and thought it would give very low odds of survival for my branch if kept in water after regrowing its roots, but to my surprise I found out that my plants and many other plants, even if not aquatic or semi-aquatic, can survive in a semi-aquatic environment.

### The roots

The broken branch, once replanted in water, began growing roots. Growth is exponential at first, taking a long time for the roots to show up from the bottom of the branch to then growing surprisingly fast and then eventually slowing down. What was very interesting to me however was the fact that these were no typical roots for a plant like this, they were aquatic roots. A branch of a tree built for soil grew perfectly-functioning aquatic roots. Unlike typical roots for soil, which are thin, hard, long and brown, these roots were instead white, translucid, thick, short and had hairs.

### Accidental hydroponics

In the end, I came to the conclusion that my plant could survive just fine on water with no soil. I could have kept it there and it would have kept living and growing, now as a semi-aquatic plant, but I decided to eventually move the plant back to soil because I don't have good conditions for hydroponics.

### Back to soil

Moving a plant with roots built for a semi-aquatic environment back to soil or vice-versa causes stress to the plant, since the current roots won't adapt well to the new environment and have to be replaced. Everything went well and my branch managed to survive easily and replace the semi-aquatic roots with the soil counterpart.

### A new tree

This plant, once a broken branch, now a tree on its own, thriving in soil and growing. Growth is a bit slow but it seems typical for this species, since they seem to adapt better to warmer environments (possibly a tropical tree).
