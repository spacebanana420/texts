# The Worst Flaws of the Education System

Most of us have suffered with the education system in one way or another, and some of us have suffered with it more than others. I have left that struggle in the past as now I am employed, but the ghost of the education system still haunts me and it's disappointing to see that this issue is not getting fixed at all, not even slightly improving, and if anything public education is spiraling down in such a dramatic way that would make accelerationists wet.

In this text I will explain what makes the education system so bad, how it affects us all and how it could have been so much better. I will also highlight some of the positive things and small improvements that can be seen in some places, such as the higher presence of project evaluation in university.

## The over-reliance on memory

Learning is primarily a matter of understanding. Memorization breeds repetition, while comprehension breeds creation. By understanding something, we can use it as a tool for developing or creating something beyond. If we merely memorize procedures, names and concepts we can only ever repeat them exactly how we memorized them prior, we become parrots.

Memorization is extremely prevalent in public education because the current education model we know came from the Prussian education system, which was designed to train obedient factory workers to follow specific procedures and protocols exactly step-by-step. This approach might be partially necessary for certain fields where repeating a procedure by memory is necessary (such as medicine) but for everything else it is the most inefficient method of learning and evaluating skill and mastery.

Everyone has experienced this at least once: you study for a test, you do the test, then the next day you remember next to nothing. This is caused by extreme over-reliance on memorization. The student, with pressure to get a good grade and induced into memorizing by how teaching and evaluation is done, superficially memorizes concepts, formulas, procedures, and the alikes in order to answer the test questions which are also superficial themselves. This short-term memory then near-instantly fades away as the usecase for having it disappears entirely, suddenly the student is no longer a "master" at the subject.

## The absence of practicality

The best and most relevant theory is the one that is put into practice. Without practical usecase, theory is only used for speculative conversation and research. Students are more motivated when they find a practical usecase for learning theory (e.g. learning about data structures in programming to understand the concept and then make your own structure) and they learn better when they put theory into practice. By putting theory into practice, you conduct your own research, notice the patterns and come up with your conclusions. You put the theory into practice and see what happens, and from that you have a much deeper understanding of the logic behind it, as you saw it with your own eyes. By putting theory into practice, it also moves on to your long-term memory due to natural consistent use of that theory in a way that is not imposed. 

## The inaccuracy of evaluation

Tests in particular tend to be extremely inaccurate at evaluating the mastery of a student in a certain field. Due to their superficial evaluation (memorizing prioritized over understanding, check box questions, etc), tests have an incredibly devastating margin of error for evaluation. Memorization is rewarded, while in-depth understanding is punished. Those who superficially memorize the procedures, tricks and concepts "milk" their grade, while those with in-depth understanding of the field often have no way to prove their mastery and knowledge in a test that relies on the student remembering names, words and formulas.

## Cheating is a symptom, not the cause

Students cheat not because they are inherently evil and treacherous but because they grow up in a highly stressful environment where their future is dictated by grades of superficial written tests in the worst ways possible. This pressure to do well and be happy in life conducts students into following whatever is the easiest and fastest path to the highest grade possible.

Students, rather than being primarily punished for trying to circumvent evaluations and restrictions that are shitty from the start, should instead be inherently discouraged to cheat, not through law enforcement and threats of getting a 0 score but by designing evaluation in a way that is not cheatable and favours creation. I will explain this right below.

## Students cheat because tests are cheatable

Cheating and teachers preventing cheating is a never-ending cat-and-mouse game. Students cheat, teachers strengthen arbitrary limitations which in result hinders honest students who do not cheat, cheaters find new ways to circumvent limitations, and the cycle repeats. This is only even possible in the first place because most tests are cheatable.

Consider the test of your native language: it has grammar questions and sometimes multiple-choice questions, these are extremely easy to cheat on, but have you ever heard of a student cheating on the written composition you have to make at the end? Questions that require insightful, in-depth, written-by-your-words answers are incredibly hard to cheat. There's no cheat sheet you can bring or formulas you can ask the student next to you to pretend you know what you are talking about. You have to **create** a real answer.

Instead of adding arbitrary limitations that hinder the accuracy of evaluating a student's mastery, tests and other evaluation methods should be designed from the start in a way that is at the very least hard to cheat and circumvent.

## Projects and written works: the better alternative to tests

Due to all the inaccuracies of written tests, an alternative way to evaluate a student's mastery of a field that is many times seen in university is projects and written works.

Projects cannot be cheated and are (usually) not superficially evaluated. You cannot memorize your way into spawning a project into this world, you have to understand the domain of your subject to then use your understanding of its theory as a tool for creation. You must conceive the project yourself using logic, understanding and creativity, and you choose which knowledge as a tool for creation of this project. For this and many other reasons (such as the absence of time anxiety), projects are much more accurate in evaluating real, hands-down practical and even theoretical mastery of a student in a field than tests, and the grades the student gets are much more predictable and consistent, less affected by external factors as well such as having a bad teacher, a bad day, luck or blanking out in a test.

Written works are a less practical alternative to projects for subjects or evaluations that cannot be put into practice nearly as much, kind of like a mini thesis. This form of evaluation is also commonly seen in university and the student, just like with projects, has to understand the field in-depth to then conceive a written work.

## Time anxiety and blanking out in tests

Students often say how they blanked out in a test. This is a very common feeling among students, but they don't associate it to a bad root cause and assume it's a natural school experience, but it is not natural at all, it is a symptom of great stress and pressure injected in a way that should never happen!

Written tests have an extremely-rigid time constraint of usually 1.5 to 3 hours. Teachers believe this makes them more accurate in filtering out students that know or understand less, probably because they think "smarter students are faster" (regardless of depth or external factors), but they couldn't have been any more wrong. Rigid time constraints affect some students more than others, and those who are not affected by this pressure are almost always the "top scorers" in grades for this reason alone, regardless of skill, mastery and intelligence.

Time pressure creates a deep anxiety in students and their brain resorts to survival "fight or flight" mode. In this state, the human brain inhibits the prefrontal cortex, which blurs cognition and inhibits working memory. Combine this with the extreme pressure of grades defining our future, and the result is a trainwreck.

This is why students blank out in the first place, and why some students who "had it all figured out" start doing the test and suddenly it's as if they knew and understood nothing. Remove this artificial time constraint and many students who fail hard in tests suddenly flourish and show deep mastery.

Out of all negative traits of written tests, this is by far the worst of them and the one I personally despised the most while I was in school.

## Do not move, do not breathe, be quiet and stand still

Another destruction of a student's mental health is the extreme authoritarian environment in classrooms. You must not stand up, you must not look to the side, you must not daydream, you must not rest and flush out your stress and exhaustion, you must not speak. We humans are not meant to stand still and be obedient machines for this long. We have to move, jump, rest, touch and feel free. The fact that we can barely even move a finger in classrooms does a big number on our mental health as students, especially boys who have a bigger necessity to move around and have physical activity.

## Homework and overworking students

In the job world, we work 8 hours a day according to our contract. With an ever-increasing focus in society for improving mental health and autonomy, work-life balance is improving in many countries especially in Europe. The moment we clock out, we toss work aside and focus on our personal life. We rightfully do not like being called or nagged outside our paid working hours and if we have downtime and are fast and efficient in our work we might even leave the office earlier.

School is the extreme opposite of this. Besides the typical 8 hours per day of school, you still come home with never-ending homework and studies. You never have time for yourself and your mental health degrades exponentially, most notably during highschool times. Ironically, homweork becomes inefficient for learning once it's just done as fast as possible so you can move on to the next one and eventually have maybe a few mintes for yourself.

Most teachers I have ever had arrogantly perceived their subject as the one and only one, incapable of understanding how overworked students already are and how they barely get by with how much homework and study they have to do exactly because every teacher thinks the other teachers don't exist.

## Convergent and divergent problem solving

Written tests are extremely rigid, relying on arbitrary problems that have to be solved in one and only way, all while in a strict time constraint. This is convergent problem solving.

Divergent problem solving is more free and autonomous. This method of thinking and solving problems focuses on real problems, and the student needs to come up with a unique way of solving it, rather than having to memorize the one and only accepted procedure. Knowledge is now a means (a tool) rather than the end, removing necessity for memorization, and different students can tackle the problem differently. This type of problem solving is dominant in project creation and written works, which are superior to written tests in every way when applicable.

## Inaccuracies in college admission

College admission is often extremely inaccurate in many countries. There is no curriculum, no interview, no evaluation of personal projects, self-taught knowledge or any written text, etc. You often won't even do an admission test tailored for that course, and instead will do a generic one (e.g maths and physics general test for computer engineering courses rather than a programming and software test). If this wasn't bad enough, your highschool grades (which are inaccurately given to you) will influence said college admission, and often all subjects will have the same weight in entry (e.g. physical education and literature having an impact in computer engineering admission).

Once again this rules out true masters of a field, prioritizing and rewarding students who cheat, milk grades and superficially memorize all fields, without having a real understanding of any.

## Teacher quality

Teacher admission itself is inaccurate, prioritizing candidates with credentials rather than those with working experience in the market and personal study. This is a much more notable problem in high school and the levels below, where teacher quality varies significantly, often for the worse.

This problem will not be fixed any time soon for another reason: the job is simply unappealing. In education, especially public schools, being a teacher is a very unappealing job: bad pay, bad working conditions, dealing with loud kids, having little autonomy, having to be allocated to a different school significantly far from your house. Why bother when you can work for a company for a significantly higher pay and benefits, all while working a hybrid or remote schedule? Private institutions don't suffer from this as much because, since they operate in the law of supply and demand, they need to provide appealing jobs for people to apply, they need to compete with rival private schools.

## You cannot pick your subjects

In school and college you are unable to make a DIY course hand-tailored to you. You cannot pick the subjects you want and are most relevant to you. You have to resort to the generic pre-made courses that come with anything and everything you hate and are uninterested about, leading to significantly higher frustration and lack of motivation. You can't even pick groups of subjects, which would have been a more realistic and pragmatic implementation of this idea.

## Credentialism and anti-merit, the rotten business

There's all kinds of learning methods and paths: theory or projects, school or self-taught, academic or professional experience, etc. We have all kinds of ways to learn and practice, and some people fit certain ways than others. Speaking for myself, my best learning path is purely and entirely self-taught, nothing else even approaches the efficiency, quality and skill of learning by myself for me.

Merit should be the only thing that ever matters for job admission: whether you can do the job and are skilled for it or not. But due to intentional stigma and misunderstanding of what makes someone competent, society relies far too much on college diplomas. This is not unintentional, by presenting college as the one and true certified education method, you can channel all motivation into college institutions, driving profits up to incredible orders of magnitude.

Once everyone feels dependant on a bachelor, employers cannot filter out candidates properly anymore. They still can by evaluating personal projects and accomplishments, but few do that. What's the next step then? To get a masters of course, so you stand out! What will happen once everyone then has a masters degree? Postgrads and certifications! Then what? Even more! What if that is not enough? Doctorates!

Of course that this is not enough to satisfy the greed of the institutions, so what else could they do? That's right, have students work internships for the college for free! Exploit their despair for getting work experience to get a job anywhere!

So many credentials everywhere and yet people are less skilled than ever. It's more than obvious by now that the possession of a diploma has little impact on a person's competence in a field, except for a few traditional courses such as medicine (even then it doesn't tell the whole picture). How many times do we see co-workers and employers note how the highschool dropout has been a much better programmer than most computer science graduates? In the end, college is a business and credentialism is a business strategy. Ask yourself, when colleges didn't exist, were people incapable of doing anything? Of course not, most countries in the world were founded and built by people without any resemblance of college education. 

## The death of in-job training

With credentialism going rampant to fuel the business of institutions, merit is now killed. This affects the merit of those with professional experience, but especially those without any professional history who are starting out. In certain fields, mastery cannot be practiced at home with personal projects or written works but it could definitely and very easily be trained on-the-job. We used to have many cases like these, even involving complex machinery. But with an ever-increasing population of job candidates and credentialism, the idea of merely being trained on-the-job without requiring a piece of academic paper is fading away.

## The academic bubble's inertia

The credentialist pre-requirements for becoming a teacher, especially a university professor, creates a survivorship bias in which the majority of active teachers will be overwhelmingly in favour of how things currently are. This stalls evolution and progress of the school system and can even make it worse over time.

## Post-school arrogance

Those who were benefited by these unfair and inaccurate schooling, evaluation and admission methods of all kinds tend to be more arrogant about it, considering people without the same credential as them as inferior, unworthy and incapable. This is not so much a school problem as it is a people problem, it vastly depends on the person in question.

## Conclusion

The education system is flawed in deep ways, from the teaching and learning methods to evaluation to admission to the very abuse of physical and mental health of the students. It's designed in an outdated way that prioritizes being obedient and memorizing procedures over true mastery and in-depth understanding of a domain followed by creation, all while creating a huge pressure on the students and consuming their whole lives until they graduate. Things are unlikely to change in any notable way for the near and possibly far future due to survivorship bias, institution business, credentialism and ignorance of the teachers, directors and education ministries.
