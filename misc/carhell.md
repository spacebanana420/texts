# Cars are The Bane of Urban Life

Cars have dominated modern life for decades in most places in the world, but at huge costs.
Their prevalence in many cities has become disastrous, but it does not have to be this way.
People begin to notice, changes need to happen, and the landscape is improving, but there is still a lot to go.

We do not have to live in car-focused concrete jungles.
Life can be so much better in a way that makes you think "wow, living in the city is truly great, population density can be good after all".


## Quick summary

The problems of car-centric infrastructure are most intense in cities. Cars take away our space, our air, our silence and our safety.
A city built for cars and not for people has most of its infrastructure and space dedicated to cars. This immediately causes multiple issues.
These issues seen below are a summary of what I talk in the next chapters:

* We barely have any space to walk ourselves, having to resort to tiny, crammed sidewalks.
* We have less space for commerce, stores, offices, housing, natural parks (and general greens) and more, as roads and parking steal all space.
* Our air is contaminated with pollution, ruining our physical and mental health.
* Quiet, tranquil cities where the most you hear is people and birds turn into noise hell, keeping our nervous system active long-term.
* Public transit without dedicated lanes becomes hindered by congestion.
* The idea of high population density changes from ideal to dystopian.
* Cycling is harder and much more dangerous when there are too many cars.
* Cities become hostile places where we need to look over our shoulders to make sure we are not run over by giant boxes of metal.
* Our wealth is hindered by the price of the car/leasing, insurance, fuel.
* Highways and many roads need constant maintenance (paid with our taxes) due to the weight of cars wearing out the floor.
* We become sedentary as drivers walk much less, and those who walk have fewer opportunities and fewer good places for it.


## Costs and inefficiency

Cars are incredibly and ever-increasingly costly. Driving lessons, driving license, buying a car or leasing, gas, insurance, highway tolls, paid parking.
In car-dominant culture, we are bound to these costs, which are overwhelmingly higher than a metro pass + bicycle.

Cars are also energetically inefficient, which is one of the reasons they ruin cities.
Cars are huge and heavy, and most of the times (especially in commuting) transport 1 person only.
The amount of space you need to transport a person is significantly more than if that person was riding a bicycle, scooter or even a motorcycle.
Public transport is also significantly more efficient with space, as a single tram can carry around 120 to 170 people on average, depending on the model.
That's 120 to 170 fewer cars on the road using the equivalent space of half a dozen of them!!!

Due to their weight, cars are incredibly energetically inefficient. The amount of kinetic energy you need to move at a certain speed increases as the weight increases.
A bicycle, weighing on average 10 to 18kg, is incredibly light when combined with 1 person, compared to a 1600kg to 3000kg car that transports 1 person.
Energy costs money and costs our environment. A metro or train is significantly heavier than a car, but transports an overwhelmingly higher amount of people, and so the energy/person ratio is very decent.


## Traffic and parking

Traffic and parking are a problem that affects everyone, but it's one of the problems drivers themselves feel the most.
Once there are too many cars, driving and parking becomes impossible as you take so long to move mere metres and the stress is huge for finding parking.


## Harm to our health and joy

Cars promote sedentarism, depression, anxiety and more.
Sedentarism comes from the strong reliance on using a car to go anywhere, as opposed to walking or cycling.
Rail infrastructure does not promote this as you still have to walk and cycle before and after you enter the metro/train.
Some people become so dependent on the poisonous comfort of the car that they drive it to travel 1 to 3km in a city!

Then we do not have a naturally healthy lifestyle that blends seamlessly with our daily life and work.
Many people then artificially induce health into our life with gym memberships and more.


## Safety for the car, harm to the person

Cars are marketed as safe, and specific models are marketed as safer than others. This is true, the car is safe for the driver and passengers, but it's very dangerous for anyone outside.

Cars are metal giants, usually at above 2000kg weight. A car at any speed above 20km/h can be considered dangerous, and accidents happen.
People, cyclists and even motorcycle drivers can be hit or run over. Cars used to be thinner, lighter and smaller, but the "safety" snowball effect began.
Newer models were made bigger and heavier, to be safer for the driver at the cost of making everyone less safe. Then, everyone else needs a big vehicle as well to feel safe.


## Cycling and dense cities

Dense cities are among the most harmed by cars for the reasons said above, but they are also among the best for cycling.
A city that is considered dense tends to have lower distances between points of interest, whether it is restaurants, home, transit, groceries, parks or general commerce.

These cities are very good for cycling, even for people not used to it. Even in countries where the car dominates, you can find people cycling in these cities.
Some do it for pleasure, others for transportation, and many for both.


## Importance of green

Green cities are more important than ever for the environment, to protect us from the sun, and to overwhelmingly make them prettier.
Walkable cities tend to be greener as they have more space for vegetation. If all our space is reserved for cars and parking, our cities become concrete jungles.


## Challenging terrain and e-bikes

Some people can argue that they do not cycle because of challenging and hilly terrain as an excuse to rely primarily on a car.
Hilly terrain is effortless to go through with public transit as it is with a car, and in a worst case scenario you can get yourself an e-bike.


## Cargo transportation

Most people can transport what they need with a bicycle. Some need as little as a basket on their bicycle, others need 2 bags at the back, and some might even need an entire cargo bike.
Regardless, cars are not commonly truly needed for cargo transportation.


## How commerce is affected

Walkable cities tend to prosper in commerce, as people who walk are much more likely to watch and enjoy their surroundings, finding new places to go and becoming customers.
This does not happen in car dominance, where no space for commerce can be found except for a few handful which are usually reserved for large companies (fueling monopoly).
Even if small companies were present, they would not prosper as they have no flesh and blood customers to find them, only cars passing by.


## How residents are affected

Residents are the most affected in the cities by cars. Noise, pollution, danger, lack of space, you name it. All the problems I mentioned above are horrible for residents.
Anyone who has ever compared a dystopian city full of cars to a green and well-developed city with nearly no cars understands this.
The immediate reaction for the former is "I would never want to live here" and for the latter "I would love living here".


## Netherlands and Denmark

Netherlands and Denmark are considered to be some of the countries with the best livable spaces and city planning in the world.
The Netherlands ranks at the top for cycling and public transit, with people strongly admiring how great it is to live exactly because of the abundance of vast and efficient public transportation combined with widespread cycling and fewer cars and parking. 


## USA

The USA is known for having one of the worst urban planning ever: concrete jungles, congestion, noise and pollution, ugly cities, weak public transit, less cycling, miserable people, fewer people walking, fewer people enjoying their home.
The common pattern is cars and their dominance ruining the rest. Since Americans are mostly unaware of how things could have been, they believe their reality has to be this way.


## Public transit snowball effect

Rail transit tends to be excellent or at the very least decent even in car-dominant cities and countries for having a dedicated lane.
Many bus lanes and some tram lanes might not have this benefit, and they might share their lane with the cars.
Becoming affected by cars, they become slow and unpredictable in their arrival on a station.
A snowball effect begins where the more cars there are, the worse public transportation without dedicated lanes becomes, and so fewer people rely on it and we have even more cars out there.
